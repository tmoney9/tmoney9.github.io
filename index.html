Design framwork

While I don't really follow any set process, there are certain pillars in every project that I try to cover.

People & problem

Typically involves wrangling information and people to gain an understanding of the problem we want to tackle. I like to end this phase with some kind of alignment document (PRD, functional spec, etc) that covers all the team's requirements and assumptions.

Structure & ideate

To get a more solid understanding of the project, I like to leverage the OOUX (Object Oriented UX) Framework. This helps the team understand the objects in our system and how they relate to one another. From there I'll jump into ideation, usually in pretty low fidelity like sketches or wireframes.

Prototype & test

Testing is the backbone of making good decisions. At this point we want to start validating our assumptions and building confidence that the thing we've built is working. Ideally we're testing with high fidelity prototypes and observing real user behaviour whilst validating it with behaviour and perception metrics.

Build & implement

Apply the learnings and principles from previous steps to create the product that we will launch to our users. My mindset shifts to become more detail-oriented, as we prepare the designs for real-world implementation. This involves a focus on ensuring that the product is not only aesthetically pleasing, but also functional, usable, and able to meet the needs of our users.

Measure & learn

Design is most effective when approached iteratively: as a cyclical process of creating and building something, testing its performance, and making improvements. This approach is less linear and more dynamic, allowing for continuous refinement and evolution of the design.

Make a payment

The Co-op Bank Payments service receives over 800,000 visits a month. The payment flow is one of the bank's core experiences, catering to both first-time and regular users.

The existing payment flow had remained largely unchanged for over 5 years, and had significant differences depending on the platform.

Initial usability testing showed that the payment flow performed relatively well with existing customers, but was "clunky" and "outdated" for customers who banked elsewhere.

The team's main hypothesis was that breaking the flow into smaller chunks would reduce cognitive load and simplify the payment process for new and repeat users.

A competitor analysis showed that there wasn't much variance in the industry regarding the general steps to make a payment, but stand-out payment journeys focused on good interaction design to improve the journey.

A task analysis was conducted to better understand the current payment flow and seek out opportunities for improvement.

The team mapped user flows for different payment scenarios, such as paying a new payee, paying an existing payee, and scheduling a payment.

A new user flow was designed that broke the service into smaller, more logical steps.

Three rounds of usability testing were conducted, with positive results.

The redesigned payment service was in line with industry conventions, scaled to different platforms, and increased user agency during the payment process.

The project took around 6 weeks from beginning to end.

The redesign laid the foundation for new functionality to be added in future iterations.

Information architecture (IA) redesign

When we started this project, the bank's web and mobile apps functioned as separate product teams with little awareness of one another. The UX team used this as an opportunity to align the IA and product strategy of both apps.

The current web and mobile apps were a tangled mess of features and services. New features got built with little thought given to findability or organization within the existing architecture.

The team conducted a tree test to determine what areas of the navigation needed improvement. 30 participants were recruited for the test, and Userzoom was used to carry it out. This helped establish a baseline for the IA to improve upon.

The test consisted of 10 tasks, each focusing on a major feature or service within the app. Userzoom scored each task under various findability related metrics, providing an overall effectiveness score for each task.

Overall results were a mixed bag. Some tasks performed relatively well, which the team wanted to preserve. Others didn't do so well.

The team analyzed the results of the tree test and past usability tests, finding some common themes. They then conducted an open card sort test to understand how users categorize and understand the products and services.

The result was a new IA that brought clarity to categories and labels, took a mobile-first approach by reducing the number of top-level categories, and rearranged categories into a flatter hierarchy, making things easier to find.

To measure the success of the redesign, the team conducted a second tree test using the new IA. The first test had an average task success of 61%, while the redesign had an average success of 87% across the same 10 tasks.

The IA redesign turned out to be a success, simplifying the architecture of the web and mobile apps, increasing findability and consistency, and making labels more in line with content guidelines and user expectations.

The approach the team took, using both qualitative and quantitative methods, gave them confidence in the results and helped them push forward to the next stage of product development.

Measuring UX at the Co-op Bank

How we created an approach to measure the effect and business impact of UX in the Co-op Bank.

By the end of 2019, we set out to tackle how we could track and measure the user experience across our products and services. Traditional metrics like NPS and conversion rates were standard currency at the bank, but these made it hard for UX to measure the impact of our work.

We created a framework for each product or service we wanted to measure, covering the following criteria:

  • Product or service purpose
  • Business or customer benefit
  • Outcomes: success and failure
  • What to measure: metrics and KPIs
  • How to measure: data source
  • Review period

We facilitated workshops with stakeholders from different products and teams within the bank. We used the framework to brainstorm ideas and voted on the most popular. The outputs got everyone on the same page and helped get stakeholders on board with the approach.

After conducting a few workshops, we created initial baselines for sales, fraud, and payments. We worked with the data analysts to create appropriate workspaces in Adobe Analytics. From here we could track our metrics and add them to our baselines.

The results of our work gave us a lot of encouragement to push forward with this approach and make it standard practice.

Future plans:

  • Using baselines to increase the visibility of UX in the bank
  • Looking across channels at multi signal metrics (like app store reviews vs NPS)
  • Measuring the effect social media or marketing campaigns have on our baseline metrics and KPIs.

Building a design system for the Co-op Bank

Before the design system, Daly, our products had no cohesive look and feel. Design files were stored locally, making collaboration and consistency a challenge. We used Sketch and Jira to move designs into development. Our design process was slow and inefficient, and our development process was costly.

We wanted Daly to tackle three major problems:

  1. the incohesive look and feel of our products,
  2. our slow inefficient design process, and
  3. our development process was costly due to duplicate efforts.

We started by gathering user feedback about our current design to validate the need for a more cohesive experience. We audited every component that made up our digital experience to show all the glaring inconsistencies. Finally, we spoke to different stakeholders to gather different points of view on how a design system could help in their daily lives.

Before diving in, we needed to decide on our core beliefs, so we came up with these five principles:

  1. tokens over specs,
  2. clarity over depth,
  3. content over graphics,
  4. feedback over expertise,
  5. and evidence over tradition.

Design tokens are a way to keep our products and services looking and feeling the same while making development faster. We stored values for typography, color, spacing, and layout as tokens. We used design tokens as a way to maintain consistency across third-party platforms.

To speed up our process, we created a component library. This Figma file houses every style and component we use in Daly. Designers can drag and drop components into their own projects without reinventing the wheel every time.

Developers used the component library, along with the design tokens, to create component repositories. These repos lived on Bitbucket, meaning other teams within the bank could use them. We used React to build components and Storybook for documentation.

Content was a big part of Daly. Backed by research, our content designers drafted guidelines on how to create, write, and use different types of content. Systemizing our content turned Daly into a pretty effective content designer.

We pitched Daly on the basis that everything we built had a solid base, a defined purpose, and was evidence-based. In the end, Daly became an expansive design system that was accessible to the entire organization. Figma was a big enabler for the bank, and we estimate that it saved us thousands of pounds in duplicate efforts.
