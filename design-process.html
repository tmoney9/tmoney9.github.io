Project 1: IA and nav redesign — Research → Prototype

Origins of this project

During the last quarter, one of our OKRs was to propose a redesign for our app's navigation. This goal arose from feedback we received during user interviews and the need to accommodate numerous new features in our roadmap.

The IA and nav redesign project aims to provide a more scalable and efficient way to find a feature or setting within our app.

Understanding user behaviour

The design process began with analysing our existing user data to better understand how they currently navigate our app. During the previous quarter, we ran qualitative user interviews with users as a collaborative journey mapping exercise. These research sessions led to a general understanding of our user's mental models and how they were using our app.

We learnt that most research participants had similar frustrations when navigating our app:

  1. Most participants found it difficult to pick up where they left off.
  2. Participants only used one or two items from the main nav, suggesting there was a bit of redundancy there.
  3. The relationships between objects didn't make sense, for example pipeline and interactive notebooks sat under projects, but cohorts didn't. There was no rationale for this.
  4. Nav items were displayed as icons, but users were unable to link the icons to specific pages or features within the app.
  5. Finally, one participant was quoted as saying “I dread having to relearn everything, because I always forget where it is.”

On top of the user frustrations mentioned above, our PM brought a couple business problems to the table:

  1. Existing architecture wasn't scalable, new features had nowhere to go.
  2. Workspace settings became a dumping ground for related and unrelated settings. This is something we wanted the redesign to solve.

Defining scope and goals

After the initial data analysis, we checked in with the project team to converge on the scope of the project and the goals we wanted to set. The team was made up of myself, a PM, a senior designer, and a UX researcher.

Rearchitecting our app and redesigning our main nav naturally meant the scope was pretty wide. However, our existing technical architecture meant our existing hierarchy needed to remain largely unchanged. In addition, we decided not to change any existing features as part of this project, instead picking them up as separate initiatives.

From here we converged on three goals we wanted this project to address:

  1. Create a scalable system: Future proof our information architecture so our software can continue to scale.
  2. Eliminate friction: Reduce the steps it takes for a user to jump back in to their previous workflow.
  3. Improve accessibility and visual design: Make it clear to users where to go and pave the way for a new visual style for the app.

Exploratory research

To gain a better understanding of the problem space, we conducted several research activities:

Heuristic analysis: we evaluated our exiting navigation against some best-practice usability principles to see how it compared.

Competitor and wider market analysis: During the first round of market analysis, we focused on how competitors and market leaders structured their information. We primarily evaluated elements such as hierarchy, labelling, and nested links.

Card sorting with existing users: We conducted several card sorting activities to help us gain a more nuanced understanding of how our users categorised the content in our app. These were run as asynchronous workshops with a range of user cohorts.

The team then gathered for another sync to discuss the findings from this phase. At this point, there were no decisions to be made beyond whether we are satisfied with the amount of research done and whether to proceed with the project.

The most relevant findings were captured as ideas for the design phase.

Designing a new app map

After deepening our understanding of the problem space, we set about modelling our own system and figuring out the relationships between the different entities (or objects) that exist within it. For this we used a technique called object mapping (from OOUX).

The purpose of this was to identify the different objects in our system, as our users understood them, and map how they relate to one another. Using this method we were able to synthesise the findings from the research phase into a clear structure with clear relationships, that aligned to our user's mental models.

Once we had structured our objects, we proceeded to map them to a new application map. The purpose of this was to visually illustrate the hierarchy of our objects. Compared to the previous app map, the proposed design had more hierarchy, which in turn made it more scalable.

We also included suggestions for labelling and moving various objects to new locations that aligned with the research findings from the previous phase.

A note here:

At this point I would usually validate this app map against our previous one using a tree test. We proposed this approach to the project manager. However, due to the project deadline, we decided to perform all of our testing during the navigation redesign phase. We had a reasonable level of confidence in our proposed redesign, which was bolstered by the research findings.

Phase 2 — Navigation redesign

The second phase of this project was to apply our redesigned app map to our product and test it. We kicked this off by going back to research, specifically looking at navigation patterns that different apps used. Our analysis mainly looked at the visual treatment of these patterns and their placement or location within the app's interface.

We mainly looked at successful SAAS applications with high user bases. The objective being to propel our own app to their level knowing that these apps are battle tested and designed by teams far bigger than our own.

Using this analysis we were able to define some guiding principles for our redesign:

  1. Preserve the sidebar navigation, these popular among data heavy apps.
  2. Use icons and labels for navigation items.
  3. Include an avatar or initials for the account link.
  4. Implement contextual navigation items based on the page or level.
  5. Ensure the first page a user sees when they log in is relevant based on where they left off or where they came from.
  6. Provide alternative ways for users to move between pages, instead of relying solely on the main navigation.

Sketching and wireframes

After agreeing on the design principles for the project, we focused on turning our ideas into something more tangible. First, we elaborated on our object maps from the previous phase by adding all the extra details within these objects:

  • Call to actions
  • User generated content
  • Metadata or system generated content
  • Nested or related objects

From there we started arranging these object maps spatially into content wireframes. This gave us the full view of all the pages we needed to cater for and a rough idea of where the content might sit on the page.

Finally we added a bit more fidelity to these wireframes to explore different nav patterns and have a more useful artefact to present to stakeholders.

Prototyping and testing

After several rounds of iterations and discussions with the PM, we made the decision to develop and test three different prototypes. To ensure that people's focus was on tasks and structure instead of visual design elements, we chose to test prototypes that were devoid of any colour or branding.

Although we developed three different prototypes, they were all built using the redesigned app map. The nuance was in the different nav patterns used to indicate the different hierarchies. Option 1 (left) proposed a more focused and stricter hierarchy, whereas Option 2 (centre), and 3 (right) facilitated quicker switching between projects and workspaces.

These were just a couple of the assumptions we wanted to validate during usability testing.

Next steps

This project is still ongoing and we're currently conducting unmoderated usability testing using Maze. The test was designed as a summative usability test so that we could benchmark prototypes against each other.

We also included some standard survey questions to evaluate such as task satisfaction and ease of use. Our aim is to test with at least twenty research participants all of which are active users. From there we'll converge on a single solution and do some more moderated usability testing.

A predictable cadence

I wanted to call out our approach to meetings throughout this project. Lifebit has somewhat of an anti-meeting culture so we set out to define the cadence and format of our meetings at the start of the project.

Instead of having meetings that aligned with the stages of the project, we opted for a more frequent cadence of bi-weekly synchs that were 15-20 minutes long. Each meeting had clear recommendations from the design team based on data gathered from previous round which fed the actions for the next round of work.

Project 2 – Detailed design and hand-off

The second project I want to mention covers the detailed design phase and implementation phase.

Drafting user stories

Usually at this point in the process, we had a clear understanding of the problem and opportunities we were going to address. With that in mind, our next step in the process was to draft user stories.

As with many organisations using SCRUM, these user stories are developer focused, and not used as a tool for ideating or exploratory design. At Lifebit we use them to capture as much detail as possible so an engineer can pick it up without much fuss.

They also ensure the team remains on the same page. From a design point of view it helps me align flows and relevant documentation to the corresponding user story.

Detailed design

The detailed design phase typically consists of designing every screen state and relevant user flow. I tend to break hand-off documents into pages and components. Generally, I will go overboard with specs and documentation as it helps me catch any missing edge cases and provides engineers with a robust starting point.

How I create hand-off documents is an ever evolving process of feedback and refinement. One thing I've found quite helpful recently is working with engineers to develop a token library. Engineers don't have to worry about hardcoding values and designers can be sure mockups are developed to spec.

Implementing the new designs

Once we've completed the detailed design phase, it's typical for us to schedule a design review session with the engineers and project managers. During this session, we can discuss any potential issues, solicit feedback, and ensure that everyone is aligned before moving forward.

This might be the first time some engineers see the mockups. How we tend to work at Lifebit is we include a representative from the tech team during ideation and design who validates the feasibility of the designs.

After delivering the work to the engineers, my focus shifts to providing design support. This role extends until the product launch, and my primary goal is to help unblock the engineers as quickly as possible.

The last part of the process before launch is the design QA step. This gives the baton back to the designer to review the implemented designs and report any gaps or issues. At Lifebit, this step ends with approval or sign-off from the designer. After which it can be moved into production.

Build, measure, learn?

A final word on our our current process at Lifebit. While we have a mechanism for capturing qualitative feedback during the early stages, we don't yet have a robust analytical framework in place to measure features after launch. This is a symptom of our current maturity and something we're working towards implementing.

The reason I mention it is, in the past, this wouldn't have been the end of the design process. Generally we'd launch features with relevant quantitive metrics that we could review at certain milestones. From there we'd make a decision to either iterate on the feature or investigate why it hasn't worked as we expected.
