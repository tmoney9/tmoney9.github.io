A new information architecture (IA)

A research driven revamp of the Co-op bank's mobile and web app information architecture.

Overview

The bank's web and mobile apps historically functioned as separate product teams with little awareness of one another. Both had their own unique roadmap despite being the same product.

The plan for 2020 was to redesign and re-platform the web app, with mobile coming shortly after. The UX team used this as an opportunity to align the IA and product strategy of both apps.

Team

Designer (me), 1 junior designer, 1 content designer, 1 ux researcher, 1 product owner.

Role

Team lead, user research, design, and project planning.

Problems

The current web and mobile apps were a tangled mess of features and services. New features got built with little thought given to findability or organisation within the existing architecture.

To increase our understanding of the problem we ran some qualitative usability testing. We conducted a tree test to determine what areas of the navigation needed improvement. The team recruited 30 participants for the test and used Userzoom to carry it out. This helped us establish a baseline for the IA on which to improve upon.

The test consisted of 10 tasks. Each task focused on a major feature or service within the app, for example: "Where would you go to remove a payee?".

Userzoom scored each task under various findability related metrics. The output was an overall effectiveness score for each task.

Screenshot from Userzoom's tree test analysis.

For the above example success was calculated at 38% with only around half of that being a direct success. Room for improvement.

Screenshot of the results for all tasks for the initial IA.

Overall results were a mixed bag. A few tasks performed relatively well, which we wanted to preserve. Others didn't do so well.

Building a better IA

The team analysed the results of the tree test, and past usablity tests, and found some common themes.

  • Unclear top level categories lead many users down the wrong path.
  • Similar or misleading categories that caused users to pick the wrong targets.
  • Categories not where users expected them to be leading to frustration and failure.
  • Unclear category labels that used terms that were unfamiliar to users.

Following this analysis the team conducted an open card sort test. The aim was to understand how our users categorise and understand our products and services.

We asked 30 users to organise features from our app and put them into groups that made sense to them.

Results from the card sort.

The result was a new IA that:

  • Brought clarity to our categories and labels by rewording them into commonly understood terms.
  • Took a mobile first approach by reducing the number of top level categories.
  • Rearranged categories into a flatter hierarchy making things easier to find.
  • Elevated popular features in the IA reducing users time to complete a task.

By reducing the hierarchy, users were able to find what they were looking for faster. A smaller global navigation meant users had less options to consider initially.

The new sitemap.

Measuring the results

To measure the success (or failure) of the redesign, we conducted a second tree test using the new IA. We tweaked each task to include the new labels the content designer had come up with.

A screenshot of the results for all tasks for the redesigned IA.

The first tree test that we baselined had an average task success of 61%. The redesign had an average success of 87% across the same 10 tasks. On top of that, the new IA was far more capable at handling new features and services down the line.

In closing

The IA redesign turned out to be a success. We simplified the architecture of our web and mobile apps, increasing findability and consistency. Additionally, labels were more in line with our content guidelines and user expectation.

The approach we took, using both qualitative and quantitative methods, gave us a lot of confidence in the results and helped us push forward to the next stage of product development.
